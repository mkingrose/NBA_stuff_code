The Starting money

It's all about the prediction game. Sports are a treasure trove of data that can help provide critical understandings and analysis about the game directly played and some of the other venues that run parallel to its presentation. We look at what past data offers to find interesting future trends and how they might give insight into those ancillary activities. This analysis provides a place to review data to understand future salaries looking at weighable stats, and determine outcomes. The data set did require some changes before it was deployable. We had to remove all players who earned a salary last year but did not play due to injury or opted out due to pandemic. It is a unique year and allows for a stress test of the system and its resiliency to adapt to an unusual data set. Then had to adjust for all players with on two-way contract that had to be removed. Those are players on minor league deals that earn $50k plus per diem and get a prorated pay raise when they are called up to play in the major league. Those players restrict to play a max of 10 games while on a two-way contract. If the team wants a more extended period, we thought it was prudent to remove those players below that threshold, train, test, split, and fit the dataframe. This would be done to get a more authentic reflection of the salary for predictive purposes and optimal lines based on player ability and salary caps.



The PER

The National Basketball Association has been in this game for a while - and understands the value of statisics. They have calucluated a score termed "Player Efficiency Rating" (hereafter PER), which measures the players' per-minute productivity. The considerations within this score is too complicated to discuss here however the scale is generally between 4 and 30 (the difference between a good high school player at low end and LeBron James setting the standard near the top). While comprehensive, the PER does not provide a lifetime judgement of the fundamental value of a player, but essentially, according to ESPN Analyst John Hollinger, "summarize a player's statistical accomplishments in a single number." And voila, a main component of our job had been done for us by those handy NBA officials. We could now get to work scoring the PER against other statistics and salary metrix.



The Stats

We calculated the salary contracted for each NBA player for the 2019-2020 season and cross-referenced them against their statisitics. These included points scored, blocks, assists, rebounds, fouls, minutes played and games played. Each of these provided additional context for the player's PER. Our Statistics webpage allows the viewer to analyze the salaries of players by team and their PER.

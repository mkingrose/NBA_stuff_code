Our Statistical Analysis

Hina Ahmed, Amber Gershman, German Portes, Mohammad Raza

Background

This endeavor was an ambitious extension of our ETL project. We wanted to use reliable, widely-available, and well-documented data to analyze and decided that sports data would be meet our criteria. Selecting the National Basketball Association proved to be interesting as the majority of our group wasn't familiar with the game, standings, players, or metrix for success. Therefore, we were able to both construct a full stack development project while learning about the game.

We began by scouring the internet for respected sources proiding information on basketball stastisics. We wanted to look at what information was available and how those datasets could be analzyed. German found NBA-Reference.com, a website providing comprehensive statistics spanning decades. Our first project sought to scrape, clean and render these tables into a SQL database.

The First Questions

Our second project began by reviewing which statistics we had included in the final dataset and how we could map and analyze those results. We began by reviewing the players' salary to deduce how much money each player earned per point scored. However, we quickly realized that - while this was a damning referendum on highly paid players in juxtaposition to our own annual salaries - the analysis would be skewed. As German pointed out, it was important to score points - but that was not the primary objective of each player. It would be unfair to judge a non-scoring player on his goals when he is primarily a shooting guard. Additionally, a player with a high salary contract who misses several games due to injury shouldn't be judged because of his convalescence.

The PER

The National Basketball Association has been in this game for a while - and understands the value of statisics. They have calucluated a score termed "Player Efficiency Rating" (hereafter PER), which measures the players' per-minute productivity. The considerations within this score is too complicated to discuss here however the scale is generally between 4 and 30 (the difference between a good high school player at low end and LeBron James setting the standard near the top). While comprehensive, the PER does not provide a lifetime judgement of the fundamental value of a player, but essentially, according to ESPN Analyst John Hollinger, "summarize a player's statistical accomplishments in a single number." And voila, a main component of our job had been done for us by those handy NBA officials. We could now get to work scoring the PER against other statistics and salary metrix.

The Stats

We calculated the salary contracted for each NBA player for the 2019-2020 season and cross-referenced them against their statisitics. These included points scored, blocks, assists, rebounds, fouls, minutes played and games played. Each of these provided additional context for the player's PER. Our Statistics webpage allows the viewer to analyze the salaries of players by team and their PER.

The Map

Additionally, we aggregated the salary total for each team and the average PER of players, and mapped out the average efficiency of teams against the size of their budget allocation. This provides a unique view, allowing the viewer to see a top-line analysis of the money spent on players versus how productive those players were, on average. Oddly enough, the teams that spend more money on players generally have a lower average PER. Obviously, this could be attributed to several different reasons - mainly that teams may allocate a huge percentage of their salary budget on someone like Lebron James or Steph Curry but due to league salary caps, would not be able to stack their remaining lineup with consistent talent. That being said - it is extremely interesting to this analyst that the three most efficient teams also spend the least.
